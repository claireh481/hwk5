Question 4¶

My results show that states that expanded Medicaid in 2014 saw substantial declines in uncompensated care, while states that never expanded saw increases over the same time period. This pattern is consistent with the Affordable Care Act’s Medicaid expansion policy, which extended Medicaid eligibility to low-income adults (up to 138% of the Federal Poverty Level) and increased enrollment through federal funding. Since coverage was expanded to previously uninsured patients, hospitals in expansion states likely treated more patients with insurance rather than providing care without payment. Thus, uncompensated care decreased because hospitals were more likely to be reimbursed through Medicaid rather than bearing the costs of treating uninsured patients.

Question 10¶

Medicaid expansion is associated with a large and statistically significant reduction in hospital uncompensated care, around $22–27 million per hospital across the different models. The event study graphs show clear declines after expansion, though the pre-treatment confidence intervals do not all include zero, particularly in 2011 for the common treatment timing event study. This initially raised minor concerns about parallel trends, however, the event study with staggered treatment timing has pre-treatment effects that are closer to zero. Overall, the evidence strongly suggests that Medicaid expansion reduced hospital uncompensated care.

A limitation of the standard TWFE approach is that it uses states that expand later as controls and combines multiple comparisons across different treatment timings. Because these groups may differ and treatment effects can change over time, this can lead to bias in the estimated effect and make it harder to interpret.
